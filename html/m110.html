Anonymity was a great possession. It gave the early

writing a general nature. It gave us the ballads; it

gave us the songs. It allowed us to know nothing of the

mans name. Anon had great privileges. He is not

resopnsible. He is not self conscious. He can borrow.

He can repeat. He can say what every one feels.

No one asks that he shall stamp his own name on his work.

He keeps at a distance from the present moment.

Anon the lyric poet repeats over and over again

that flowers fade; that death is the end. He is never

tired of celebrating red roses and white breasts.

The anonymous playwright has like the singer this nameless

vitality, something drawn from the crowd in the penny

seats and not yet dead in ourselves. For we too become

anonymous and forget something that we have learnt

in reading the plays that no one has troubled to set a

name to.


But at some point there comes a break when anonymity

withdraws. It comes presumably when the playwright has

absorbed the contribution of the audience; and returns it

to them in a single figure which is individual. There is a

moment in play after play when the audience is no longer

master of the playwright; yet is not separate from him.

A common life still unites them, but the playwright becomes

increasingly diminant. This separation comes fitfully.
